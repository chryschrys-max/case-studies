Most Vulnerable Wells

Sleep and Bond reach critical status by Upper Sec because they have no clear driver after parental enforcement ends. Screen drives both declines but is itself driven by emotional function — creating a reinforcing loop with no natural exit point.

Highest Leverage Points

The Sec 2→3 transition (14–15yo) is where parental influence dips below peer influence and habits entrench. Interventions at this window have the best chance of shifting trajectories before patterns lock in.

The Handover Problem

Every well follows the same arc: parent-structured → eroding → no one owns it. The gap isn't just parental withdrawal — it's that no alternative driver replaces parental structure. Youth need skills and systems, not just reminders.
Cross-Well Interactions That Accelerate Decline
1
Screen → Sleep → Everything. The dominant cascade. Screen displaces sleep, poor sleep disrupts eating (skip breakfast, snack late), fatigue suppresses exercise. One slip pulls three others. Appears in 8/11 youth interviews.
2
Bond erosion removes the last check on Sleep. When parent-youth communication breaks down, the last external prompt for bedtimes disappears. Sleep becomes entirely self-managed — but no one taught self-management.
3
Screen replaces Bond as coping mechanism. Youth who don't turn to parents when stressed use phones instead. This reinforces screen dependency while further weakening the relationship that could help break the cycle.
4
Academic pressure is the invisible multiplier. It doesn't directly damage any single well — it compresses the time and energy available for all of them, making every healthy choice harder and every unhealthy default more likely.
