We tested three concepts individually, but the most useful findings cut across all of them. These six patterns should shape whatever goes into the pilot.

Bottom line
Six patterns emerged across all three concepts. They tell us what youth respond to, what parents actually need, and what falls apart when concepts meet real life.
1
Youth motivation is real, but it runs on identity and social currency, not health outcomes

Youth who engaged most strongly did so because the concept connected to something they already cared about: looking good, competing with friends, or understanding their parents better. Nobody said "being healthier" as a reason to use any of the three concepts.

"It has so many ways to like change yourself without buying products and everything. Like it helps you change yourself naturally."TY01 Evangeline, 13 — Validation
"It's kind of like Snapchat or TikTok streaks — you don't want to break the streak."TY06 Kayna, 13 — Validation
For the pilot: If it sounds like a health pamphlet, it will sit in a drawer. If it sounds like something their friends are doing, it has a chance.
2
Parents want something they can point to, not another rule to enforce

Parents who responded most strongly were the ones whose usual approach had started to backfire. They were not looking for another rule. They wanted something external that did the work for them: a board that makes the conversation visible, a challenge run by a friend so the parent does not have to push, a card game where the questions come from a deck instead of from mum or dad.

"This is something we have been looking for a long time."TP12 Bhavani — Validation
"I always say the same thing. Go sleep early. Go and study. I don't think he gets my message."TP06 Han Tien — Validation
"I tried to change before, but the boys become more rebellious."Ryan, Parent — Co-creation
For the pilot: Give parents a shared object or ritual that replaces the nag. Not another thing for them to manage, but something that does the reminding on their behalf.
3
Nothing lasted beyond two weeks without a reason to come back

Every group raised this independently. Youth, parents, younger, older. Everyone said the same thing: interesting for a week or two, then it stops. No concept had anything built in to bring people back once the novelty wore off.

"Will it be consistent for a period? I don't think so."TP07 Julie — Validation
"Kids will do it for 1 week and won't continue."TP09 Lee Ee Cheng — Validation
"I will keep doing it until I lose interest and forget."TY11 Zillia, 14 — Validation
For the pilot: Sustainability is the core design problem. Every part of the pilot needs a reason to come back, something that changes week to week, and a way to restart without feeling like you failed.
4
Concepts that felt like "my thing" worked. Ones that felt handed down did not.

Youth clearly separated what felt like their own from what felt adult-driven. C2 scored highest because youth chose the topic, chose the buddy, and ran the challenge themselves. Where anything felt assigned, engagement dropped.

"I wouldn't feel pressure to do it because like if I really didn't want to do it, I wouldn't do it. But I want to do it. So like that's why I'm doing it."TY01 Evangeline, 13 — Validation
"Once take back, I will go back to my sneakier behaviour!"Amanda, 15 — Co-creation
For the pilot: Youth must feel ownership from the first interaction. Distribution through parents or schools is fine, but the first touch should feel chosen, not assigned.
5
Trust is the precondition. Without it, no parent-youth concept works.

The same design feature (feelings magnets) was read as surveillance by one youth and as a communication lifeline by another. The difference was not the design. It was the relationship. Trust at 13 was described as "make or break" in co-creation, and validation confirmed it.

"Invasion of privacy — I wouldn't want my parents knowing I'm feeling sad or angry."TY04 Xi En, 14 — Validation
"Working on trust is very important. If this stage (age 13) is broken then it's very hard to counter in the future."Jemimah, Parent — Co-creation
For the pilot: The pilot needs a trust-building entry point before anything that involves parent-youth interaction. Start with low-stakes, practical topics. Go deeper only after trust is built.
6
Every concept worked better at the start of a transition than in the middle of one

Participants who were early in a transition responded more openly than those already settled into patterns. Where structure had already collapsed, the board was "too late." Where routines were already solid, the concept was "unnecessary." The sweet spot is the window when old structures are falling away but new ones have not formed yet.

"Too fixed and boring."TP10 Yew Pei Ling — Validation (daughter already self-managing)
"Feel it is too late now to set a rule."Calvin, Parent — Co-creation
For the pilot: Timing matters as much as the concept. Deploy at transition points, not generically. This is what the building blocks are designed for.

Part 3

TTM, Transitions & How We Make It Last

The Transtheoretical Model tells us where someone sits in their readiness to change. Each transition maps to a different range of readiness. Concepts are matched to transitions. Building blocks are matched to concepts.

Why this matters
In validation, the same concept landed completely differently depending on where the participant sat. TP12 was ready to start and asked for a copy on the spot. TP10 already had her routines sorted and found everything pointless. Offering the wrong thing at the wrong time does not work, no matter how well it is designed.

The sustainability plan: four phases

Every participant told us the same thing: interesting for two weeks, then it stops. The plan has to answer what happens in week three, week six, and month three.

Phase 1
The Spark
Week 1 to 2

Novelty does the heavy lifting. The first step is so easy that skipping it feels harder than doing it. A physical cue is placed in the environment so nobody has to remember to start. Fun and surprise keep attention through the first few sessions. The goal is not lasting change. It is a small win that builds confidence for the next step.

Design moves
First step is one action, one day. Ridiculously easy.
Physical cue placed on day one: board beside bed, card on table, sticker on phone.
Each day feels different. A new card, a new challenge, a new question.
Immediate payoff: a sticker, a streak counter, a parent acknowledgement.
"I actually feel motivated to do this. This game looks quite like interesting."TY01 Evangeline, 13 — first reaction to C2
Phase 2
The Bridge
Week 3 to 6

Novelty has worn off. This is the danger zone everyone predicted. What carries the habit now is the social layer. The buddy asking "did you do yours?" is the prompt. The streak that would break is the motivator. A weekly review replaces daily novelty with a rhythm. The parent shifts from "did you do it?" to "how did it go?"

Design moves
Buddy check-in becomes the primary prompt. The tool steps back, the people step forward.
Weekly reset: review what worked, swap what did not, pick a new focus.
Parent role shifts from enforcer to ally. Ask "how did it go?" not "did you do it?"
"Light day" option for CCA days, exam weeks, and low-energy days.
"Kids will do it for 1 week and won't continue."TP09 Lee Ee Cheng — the problem this phase solves
Phase 3
The Shift
Month 2 to 3

Youth who are still engaged have started to see themselves differently. The challenge is no longer something they do. It is part of who they are. Visible progress reinforces this: a before-and-after, a six-week streak, a reflection on what changed. Youth start customising the concept to fit their life: choosing different decks, setting their own goals, inviting new buddies.

Design moves
Reflection moment: "what's different now compared to when you started?"

Phase 4
The Anchor
Month 3 onwards

The tool can step back. The habit is attached to a routine anchor: after dinner, before bed, on the walk home. The youth can explain to a friend why they do this. That is the strongest sign the behaviour is internalised. The concept's job is not to keep running. It is to have built something that continues without it.

Design moves
Habit is stacked onto an existing routine. The tool is no longer needed to trigger it.
Youth can share the concept with others. Passing it on is the strongest commitment.
Parent role has shifted permanently. The new dynamic does not depend on the tool.
The concept has a natural "graduation" moment. Completion, not abandonment.
"When I go poly, I'll be on my own. My parents won't know what I eat, when I sleep."Y11, 16 — Discovery (the habit needs to survive this moment)
