Mapping the Machine: Pre-Discovery Systems Scoping for a Complex Public Sector Platform

Before you can redesign a system, you have to understand what it actually is. This is the story of a one-month scoping engagement that mapped four interconnected systems, charted every capability, and identified where the real complexity lives — so that discovery could begin with eyes open.

ClientSingapore Government Agency (Public Sector)
PhasePre-Discovery Scoping (1 Month)
TeamLead Designer, Principal BA, Solutions Architect
RoleLead Designer / Design Strategist
The Brief

Four systems. One month. Everything as-is.

The programme goal was to modernise a complex case management platform used across a major public sector operation. Before any discovery work could begin — before we could ask users what wasn't working or imagine what better looked like — we needed to understand the terrain.

That meant mapping what currently existed across four systems: what each one did, what it was capable of, and critically, where and how they connected to one another. This phase — which we called the scoping phase — was designed to be a pre-discovery exercise. Its purpose was to make us better prepared to plan and execute the real discovery that would follow.

What I did

I led the design and facilitation work within a cross-functional team of three — a Principal BA and a Solutions Architect alongside me. Each of us brought a different lens to the same systems.

  • Led engagement design — structured how we would systematically move through each system, who we needed to speak to, and how to capture capability information consistently across four different environments
  • Facilitated stakeholder sessions across system owners and operational teams to surface how each system was actually used day-to-day, not just what it was built to do
  • Synthesised across disciplines — translated technical architecture insights from the Solutions Architect and process documentation from the Principal BA into a picture a non-technical audience could act on
  • Mapped the handshakes — identified and documented every point where one system passed work to another, including the informal and manual bridges that had grown up over time
  • Framed the output as a discovery brief — turned raw mapping into a structured foundation that the discovery phase could build from, with clear questions, known unknowns, and areas of risk flagged

Context

Why scope before discovery?

In most HCD projects, discovery is where you start. You go in, talk to users, and let the problems reveal themselves. But when the environment is a multi-system, multi-stakeholder public sector operation, going in blind has a real cost: you lose weeks asking questions that a better-prepared team would already know the answers to.

The scoping phase inverted the typical approach. Instead of beginning with users, we began with the systems. The goal was not to find problems to solve — that was discovery's job — but to establish a baseline. What exists. How it works. Where it connects. What the constraints are. What we'd be designing within.

The logic behind scoping first

A discovery team that walks in without a map of the systems will spend the first few weeks building that map. A discovery team that walks in with a map can spend those same weeks talking to users about their experience of those systems. The scoping phase was designed to give discovery that head start — not to answer discovery's questions, but to make sure discovery was asking the right ones from day one.

What made this complex

Four systems sounds manageable on paper. In practice, complexity compounds quickly: each system has its own data model, its own user base, and its own operational history. More importantly, the real complexity wasn't within any single system — it was between them. How a case moved from one system to the next, what triggered a handoff, who was responsible at each stage, and where things fell into gaps: that's what we were really mapping.

The Core Work

Mapping the handshakes

In complex multi-system environments, the handshake — the moment where one system passes responsibility to another — is often where the real problems live. Not inside any one system, but in the gaps between them.

Mapping the handshakes meant going beyond what the technical documentation said should happen, to understand what actually happened: the informal workarounds, the manual steps that had become standard practice, the data that got rekeyed because two systems didn't talk to each other, and the decisions that got made in those gaps that no one had formally documented.

Why handshakes matter for discovery

A discovery team focused only on individual systems would miss the real friction — which almost always lives at the boundaries. By mapping handshakes in the scoping phase, we could tell the discovery team: these are the moments where the case management process is most likely to break, slow down, or generate workarounds. Start your user research here. That's the kind of input that turns a generic discovery into a targeted one.

Team & Approach

Three disciplines, one picture

The scoping team was deliberately cross-functional. Each discipline contributed a different layer of understanding — and the synthesis across all three is what made the output useful for discovery.

Lead Designer

Design & Facilitation

Structured the engagement, facilitated stakeholder sessions, and synthesised across disciplines into a picture that non-technical stakeholders could act on. Led handshake mapping and framed outputs for discovery.

Principal BA

Business Analysis

Documented business processes, capability inventories, and data flows across each system. Surfaced the operational logic behind how cases moved through the organisation.

Solutions Architect

Technical Architecture

Assessed the technical landscape: integration patterns, system constraints, legacy dependencies, and architectural decisions that would shape what modernisation was actually possible.

What the cross-functional model gave us

Working across design, BA, and architecture meant we could triangulate. When the BA documented a process and the Solutions Architect mapped the technical integration, I could sit across both and ask: does the system actually support the process it's supposed to enable? More often than not, the answer was "partly" — and that gap became a signal for discovery to follow up on.

Reflections

What I learned about working in complex systems

01

The gaps between systems are more revealing than the systems themselves

Every system had documentation. None of the handshakes did. The informal processes, manual bridges, and workarounds that had grown up at integration points were invisible until we specifically went looking for them. That's where the real complexity — and the real opportunity for improvement — lives.

02

What a system does and what it's used for are not the same thing

Across all four systems, there was a consistent gap between designed intent and actual use. Systems had capabilities that were never used, and operational needs that were being met by manual workarounds because no system had been built to handle them. Documenting both — not just one — is what makes a capability map useful.

03

A cross-functional team sees things a single discipline misses

Working alongside a Principal BA and Solutions Architect meant I could surface mismatches that neither discipline would have caught alone: processes that the BA documented but the Solutions Architect knew the system couldn't support; technical constraints that shaped operational workarounds that had never been flagged as a problem. The synthesis across disciplines is where the insight was.

04

Scoping is an investment, not overhead

On a project this complex, going straight to discovery without a systems map would have been expensive. The scoping phase cost one month. In exchange, the discovery team inherited a clear picture of the environment, a targeted set of questions, and an explicit register of risks and unknowns. That's not a delay — it's preparation that pays back across every phase that follows.

Outcome

What the scoping phase made possible

We went into the scoping phase with four systems, a brief to modernise, and an unknown amount of complexity. We came out of it with a full as-is picture of the current environment, a documented map of every integration point across the four systems, a clear set of questions for discovery to build from, and a team that was genuinely prepared for what came next.

The value of this phase wasn't in the artefacts alone — it was in the shared understanding they represented. When discovery began, the team didn't need to spend weeks getting oriented. We could go straight to the work that matters: talking to users about their actual experience of these systems, and using what we already knew about the environment to ask better questions.

The broader point

Complex systems require a different kind of preparation. The skills that make HCD work in a focused, single-product context — going in early, staying open, letting users lead — don't disappear in a multi-system environment. But they need to be grounded in something. You can't design your way into clarity if you don't know what system you're designing within. This project taught me to see that preparation as part of the design work, not a precursor to it.
