Designing the Crowd: Behavioural Design Before I Knew Its Name

Working on the concert-goer experience at a major sports and entertainment venue, I was already practising behavioural design — ethnographic observation, contextual inquiry, choice architecture — before I had the language for it. This is where it started.

ClientMajor Sports & Entertainment Venue
DomainLive Events • Venue Experience
MethodBehavioural Design (in hindsight)
RoleUX Designer
The Brief

55,000 people, one venue, zero time to think

The venue hosts some of the largest live events in Southeast Asia — concerts, sports, festivals — with tens of thousands of people moving through a complex physical and digital environment. The brief started with wayfinding: how do you get people where they need to go in a venue this size? But it quickly expanded into the full end-to-end event experience — from the moment someone discovers an event to the moment they leave the venue.

The design challenge wasn’t just usability. It was about understanding how people actually behave when they’re in a crowd, running late, overstimulated, and making decisions on instinct. Wayfinding was the entry point, but the real problem was the entire concert-goer journey.

At the time, I didn’t call it behavioural design. I called it “figuring out what people actually do.” But looking back, every method I used maps directly to established behavioural science frameworks — and this project became the foundation for everything I’ve done since.

What I did

I worked on the concert-goer experience — the end-to-end journey from discovering an event to leaving the venue. My focus was on understanding real behaviour in context, then designing around it.

  • Observed concert-goers in the venue — watched how people navigated the space, where they got lost, where they clustered, and what decisions they made under pressure
  • Conducted on-site interviews — spoke with attendees during events to understand their decisions in the moment, not in retrospect
  • Built concert-goer personas — modelled the different ways people think, plan, and react across event types and experience levels
  • Mapped decision points across the venue — identified where the physical environment was shaping behaviour, for better or worse
  • Designed wayfinding and information touchpoints — used spatial and digital design to guide behaviour through the environment rather than relying on instructions people would never read
Research

On-site observation, contextual interviews, behavioural mapping, crowd flow analysis

Synthesis

Persona development, journey mapping, decision-point mapping, environment audit

Design

Wayfinding systems, information architecture, touchpoint design, spatial UX

The Frameworks (In Hindsight)

I was doing behavioural design before I knew the name

Looking back at this project through the lens of what I now know, every instinct I followed maps to an established framework. I wasn’t reading Kahneman or Thaler at the time. I was just trying to solve real problems for real people in a chaotic environment. But the methods were already there — I just didn’t have the vocabulary yet.

Ethnographic Research

Observing behaviour in natural context

I spent time in the venue during live events — not running tests in a lab, not sending surveys after the fact. I watched how people actually moved through the space: where they hesitated, where they followed others, where they ignored signage entirely. The venue was the research environment.

What I called it then: “Just watching what people do.”

Contextual Inquiry

Understanding decisions in the moment they happen

I didn’t wait until after the event to ask people what they thought. I spoke with concert-goers while they were navigating the experience — in queues, at decision points, when they were lost. Their answers in the moment were completely different from what they would have said the next day.

What I called it then: “Talking to people while they’re doing the thing.”

Persona Development

Modelling how a concert-goer thinks and decides

Not all concert-goers behave the same way. The first-timer who arrives two hours early and reads every sign is a fundamentally different user from the regular who navigates on muscle memory. I built personas not around demographics, but around decision-making patterns — how different people process information and make choices under pressure.

What I called it then: “Different people do this differently.”

Choice Architecture

How the physical environment shapes where people go

The venue layout wasn’t neutral. Where you placed a food stall, how you oriented a barrier, which path was wider — all of it shaped where 55,000 people ended up. I mapped how the physical environment was making decisions for people, then redesigned those decision points so the default path was the right one.

What I called it then: “Making the obvious thing the right thing.”

Wayfinding as Nudge

Directing behaviour through design, not instruction

You can’t put a 10-step instruction list in front of someone who’s about to see their favourite artist. Wayfinding had to work without being read — through spatial cues, visual hierarchy, and environmental design that guided people without them realising they were being guided. The best wayfinding is invisible.

What I called it then: “If they have to read it, we’ve already failed.”

System 1 Thinking

Concert-goers aren’t deliberating — they’re reacting

The single most important insight from this project: people at live events are not making rational, deliberate decisions. They’re operating on instinct, emotion, and environmental cues. They follow the crowd. They go where it feels right. They don’t read signs — they read the room. Every design decision had to work for a brain that wasn’t really thinking.

What I called it then: “Nobody reads anything.”

Looking back

This project taught me something I didn’t fully understand until years later: the best design doesn’t ask people to change their behaviour — it works with the behaviour that’s already there. I wasn’t trying to educate concert-goers or make them more rational. I was designing for how they actually think. That instinct — design for real behaviour, not ideal behaviour — is what eventually led me to behavioural design as a discipline.

Why This Matters

The origin story

This project didn’t feel like a turning point at the time. It felt like a venue design project. But it planted the methods that would define how I work today.

When I later encountered formal behavioural design frameworks — COM-B, nudge theory, the EAST framework — I recognised them immediately. Not because I’d studied them, but because I’d already been doing them. This venue project gave me the instincts. The theory gave me the language.

From instinct to method

At This venue project, I observed behaviour because it was the only way to understand what was happening. I built personas around decision patterns because demographics didn’t explain anything. I designed nudges because instructions didn’t work. These weren’t conscious methodological choices — they were responses to the problem in front of me. Recognising them later as established frameworks gave me the confidence to apply them deliberately, and the vocabulary to explain why they work.

From venue to everything

The core insight — design for how people actually behave, not how you wish they would — transferred directly into every project that followed. A youth health project uses the same principle: don’t tell teenagers to be healthy, understand why they drift and design around it. A design operations engagement uses the same principle: don’t give designers a process document, build a system that fits how they actually work. It all started here.

Reflections

What I took forward

01

Observe first, design second

You cannot design for behaviour you haven’t witnessed. Surveys and interviews tell you what people think they do. Observation tells you what they actually do. The gap between those two things is where the real design opportunities live.

02

Context changes everything

A person filling out a form at home and a person navigating a venue with 55,000 others are not the same user, even if they’re the same person. Context — stress, time pressure, sensory overload, social influence — fundamentally changes how people process information and make decisions.

03

The environment is the interface

In a venue, the physical space is the product. Where you place things, how you orient paths, what you make visible and what you hide — these are design decisions with the same weight as any screen layout. Choice architecture isn’t just a digital concept.

04

You don’t always need the theory first

I didn’t need to read about System 1 thinking to know that concert-goers weren’t reading signs. The practice came first. The theory came later and gave me language for what I was already doing. Sometimes the best way to learn a framework is to discover you’ve been using it all along.
