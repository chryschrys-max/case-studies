Flipping the Drift: Behavioural Design for Youth Health in Singapore

How we used behavioural science — not just service design — to uncover why Singaporean youth silently lose their healthy habits, and designed three physical interventions to flip the pattern.

01 — The Challenge

Youth aren't choosing to be unhealthy. The structures holding their habits just... disappear.

Singapore's Health Promotion Board had strong data on older youth (17–25), but the 13–16 age group — the secondary school years — was a blind spot. National health metrics were declining across the board. HPB needed to understand what was happening in this critical window before they could act.

The Thinking Process

This project could have been a conventional CX/UX exercise — map the journey, identify pain points, propose service improvements. We deliberately chose not to do that. The numbers told us this wasn't a touchpoint problem. Youth weren't having bad experiences with health services; they were drifting away from healthy habits before any service could reach them. That meant we needed to understand behaviour, not experience. We brought in COM-B, transition analysis, and behavioural ecosystem mapping — tools from behavioural science — because the answer wasn't in optimising a service journey. It was in understanding why behaviours change when the structures around young people shift.

02 — Our Approach

Behaviour science meets human-centred design

We didn't start with personas or journey maps. We started with a behavioural ecosystem — mapping 7 behaviours across 7 developmental stages to find where and why habits break down. This is what separates behavioural design from traditional service design.

Phase 1 — Discover
Behavioural Ecosystem Blueprint
24 in-depth interviews (12 youth, 12 parents, 2 experts). We mapped 7 behaviours across 7 stages, applied COM-B analysis to every barrier, and scored all 6 Wells across each developmental transition. This wasn't about "how was your experience?" — it was about "why did this behaviour change?"
Phase 2 — Define
Transition Zone Mapping & DVF Prioritisation
Internal workshop with HPB using HMW methodology. We identified 17 opportunity areas, scored them against 18 DVF criteria (Desirability, Viability, Feasibility), and pinpointed the two critical vulnerability windows: after-school (2–6pm) and nighttime (9pm–1am+).
Phase 3 — Co-create
25 Ideas Down to 3 Behavioural Interventions
Separate co-creation workshops with 6 youth and 6 parents. Day-in-the-life mapping, stimulus testing, gallery walk voting. From 25 directions, we shortlisted 3 concepts using behavioural leverage criteria — not just appeal.
Phase 4 — Validate
Prototype Testing with 24 Participants
Individual 60-minute moderated sessions with physical prototypes. 7-point Likert scales on 5 dimensions. Think-aloud protocol. Testing not just "do you like it?" but "would this change your behaviour?"
Why This Matters

Traditional HCD would have us map a youth health "journey" and find service gaps. But there is no journey here — there's a drift. Youth don't wake up one day and choose to stop sleeping well. They transition into secondary school, lose the structures that held their habits, and slowly slide. That's a behavioural phenomenon, not a service design problem. So we built behavioural analysis tools — ecosystem blueprints, COM-B barrier maps, transition zone scoring — and used them to find the leverage points. The team learned to ask "what behavioural mechanism is at work?" before jumping to solutions.

03 — Key Insights

The Drift: how healthy habits silently unravel

The central finding was what we called "the drift" — the gradual, unintentional slide away from healthy habits during transitions. Not because youth choose to stop, but because the structures that held those habits disappear before they can self-manage.

The drift manifests in three interconnected breakdowns:

1

Knowing to Not Doing

Youth know what's healthy. They were never taught how to do it on their own — someone else always managed it for them.

2

Structure to Gap

Bedtimes, family meals, supervised afternoons all decrease by Sec 1. What replaces them defaults to convenience, not health.

3

Guidance to Friction

Parents haven't stopped caring. But their tools — monitoring, rules, reminders — now trigger resistance rather than compliance.

The Thinking Process

This is the slide that changed the project. When we laid out COM-B side by side — youth and parents — we saw they were describing the same problem from opposite sides. Both are trying. Both are failing. Not because of a missing touchpoint or a bad experience, but because the behavioural system shifted and neither side has the tools to adapt. A UX lens would have said "improve the communication." A behavioural lens says "the communication itself is fine — the strategies available to both sides are mismatched to the developmental stage." That's a fundamentally different design brief.

04 — The Solution

Three physical interventions. No apps.

All three concepts are intentionally physical — not digital. A phone in the bedroom is the problem (Bedtime Wind-Down). After-school time is already screen-dominated (Hook U Up). Face-to-face conversation is the whole point (WOU). These aren't touchpoints in a service — they're behavioural scaffolds designed to fill the gap where structure used to be.

01

Autonomy with Guardrails

Design for shared structure, not control. The youth owns the choice; the framework holds the edges.

02

Replace, Don't Restrict

Never remove a behaviour without introducing an alternative. Removing phones without substitutes leads to workarounds.

03

Fit the Day They Already Have

Strengthen existing behaviours before introducing new ones. Don't add to the load — reshape what's already there.

Bedtime Wind-Down

Physical magnet board for the fridge
Youth design their own bedtime routine on a fridge magnet board. They pick wind-down activities (warm shower, chill music, dim lights) and room setup (phone outside room, alarm set). Parent has a visible commitment column. Weekly review. Framed as a "5-night experiment, not a rule."

Hook U Up

14-day buddy challenge card deck
Physical deck of challenge cards done in buddy pairs. Five topic decks named after what youth care about: Glow Up (skin), Bulk Up (strength), Cut Season (energy), Recharge (less tired), Energy Era (mood). Daily card flip. QR proof upload.

Parent-youth conversation card game
22 cards: 16 category cards (both answer), 2 swap cards (answer as each other), 4 spark cards (go deeper). Questions drawn from real research tensions. Uses "For Now" framing to make answers feel temporary. Designed to side-step the lecture dynamic.

The Thinking Process

The decision to go physical was a direct output of behavioural analysis, not a creative preference. When we mapped the two critical windows — after-school (2–6pm) and nighttime (9pm–1am) — digital devices were the common thread. Adding another app would have been adding to the problem. The COM-B analysis showed that the opportunity gap was environmental, not informational. So we designed environmental interventions: a board on the fridge (visible, shared), cards on the table (social, tangible), a game face-to-face (relational, not transactional). Each concept targets a specific COM-B gap rather than trying to be a general "health solution."

Key recommendations delivered to HPB:

Design for the gap, not the school day

HPB's current touchpoints sit in the structured school window (8am–1:30pm). The drift happens in the unsupervised windows. Solutions must work when parents can't see.

Don't lead with "health"

Lead with what matters now: energy, confidence, appearance, social credibility. Health is the outcome, not the hook.

Parents need a different playbook

Shift from enforcers to facilitators. The relationship itself becomes the structure, not a set of rules.

Peers will shape choices in the gap

If habits are to stick, the healthier option must be easier in friend settings. Peer influence is the primary lever after Sec 2.

06 — Reflection

Why this is a behaviour study, not a service design project

The instinct in human-centred design is to map journeys, find friction, and optimise touchpoints. That works when someone is using a service and having a bad experience. But these youth weren't having a bad experience — they were drifting without noticing. There was no "service" to fix. The problem was behavioural: habits eroding as environmental structures shifted.

We made a conscious decision early to frame this as a behavioural study. That meant:

COM-B before personas

We mapped Capability, Opportunity, and Motivation barriers before we built any personas. The personas emerged from the behavioural analysis, not the other way around.

Ecosystem before journey

We built a behavioural ecosystem (7 behaviours x 7 stages) before any journey map. This revealed the cascade effect — something a linear journey would have missed.

Transition zones, not touchpoints

Instead of mapping service touchpoints, we mapped developmental transitions. The drift doesn't happen at a touchpoint — it happens in the gaps between them.

Behavioural leverage, not desirability

Concepts were evaluated on whether they address a specific COM-B gap, not just whether users liked them. DVF scoring included behavioural criteria alongside feasibility.

The Shift

The biggest shift wasn't methodological — it was mindset. The team moved from asking "what's the user's pain point?" to asking "what behavioural mechanism is driving this pattern?" That one question changed everything: the research design, the analysis framework, the concept evaluation criteria, and ultimately the kind of solutions we built. A CX project would have designed a better health app. A behavioural project designed a magnet board, a card deck, and a conversation game — because those are what the behavioural analysis said would actually work.
