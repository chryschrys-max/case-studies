Case Study

Flipping the Drift: Behavioural Design for Youth Health in Singapore

A human-centred design project with Singapore's Health Promotion Board to understand why healthy habits decline among youth aged 13–16 — and to co-design interventions that work within their real lives.

ClientHealth Promotion Board (HPB), Singapore
DurationDec 2025 – Mar 2026
MethodHuman-Centred Design (4 Phases)
RoleLead Designer / Researcher
The Brief

Why do healthy habits decline between ages 13 and 16?

HPB tracks youth health across 6 "Wells" — Eat, Sleep, Exercise, Learn, Bond, and Screen Time. The data showed a consistent pattern: health behaviours declined as youth moved through secondary school. But HPB didn't know why.

The brief was to investigate the age group 13–16, understand the drivers behind the decline, and design interventions grounded in what youth and their families actually experience — not what adults assume they need.

Phase 1 — Discover

The drift: why healthy habits quietly slip away

The research identified a pattern we called "the drift" — the unintentional slide away from healthy habits, not because youth choose to stop, but because they haven't yet developed the skills to manage it themselves. The structures that held their habits in place simply disappear.

What the research told us

Youth know what healthy looks like and most want it. But three things keep getting in the way: (1) knowing without the skills to do it alone, (2) structured routines fading while choices multiply, and (3) parents stepping back before youth are ready, creating distance instead of independence.

The research mapped this drift across three key transition zones where habits break:

Best window to intervene

P6 → Sec 1

The Big Handover. Student Care ends. Youth gets a phone, a house key, and pocket money. Afternoons shift from adult-supervised to self-managed — but the habits maintained by external structures haven't been replaced by skills to manage them independently.

"If this stage broken then very hard to counter in the future." — Parent
The leverage window

Sec 2 → Sec 3

The Deepening Drift. Peer influence becomes the dominant force. Fast food becomes a daily social ritual. Conversations with parents narrow to reminders. 8 out of 11 youth and 5 out of 10 parents described the same cascade: screen time up, sleep down, eating suffers, exercise drops.
Outside target scope

Sec 4 → JC / IHL

The Fork. Youth who built self-management strategies carry them forward. Those stuck in the vicious cycle carry those patterns into adulthood. What we build for Sec 1–3 determines which path youth take.

"When I entered Sec 1, everything changed. New school, new friends, new timing. I had to figure everything out myself."

Y03, 15yo Male

"In primary school, everything was structured. Dinner at 7, homework by 8, bed by 9:30. The routine held. Then secondary school started..."

P04, parent of 14yo & 16yo sons

→ Help youth buy into the routine, not just follow it.

Supported & Functioning

Routines are in place and youth follow them naturally. But during primary school, habits are held up by people around them, not by the youth themselves.

Discover — Opportunity Areas

What youth, parents, and the ecosystem each need

The research surfaced four opportunity spaces — what each group needs to make the healthy choice easier.

Youth

"Small steps that fit my routine"

"When I am tired after school or at night, I need small steps that fit my routine so the healthy choice feels easy."

Parent

"Supportive without nagging"

"When I cannot see what my youth is doing, I need ways to stay supportive without nagging or spying."

Parent + Youth

"Connect without lecturing"

"When we are together, we need a way to connect that doesn't turn into a lecture. When apart, I need to trust they have the tools."

Key Supporters

"Make the default easier"

"When youth are in school and hangout spaces, the default environment should make healthier routines easier to start and repeat."

Discover + Co-creation — The Two Windows

Where unhealthy choices quietly build up

In primary school, adults structured the whole day. In secondary, two large windows open up where unhealthy choices happen without any adult awareness or support.

Morning
6:30 – 7:30am
Youth wakes to phone alarm, scrolls before getting up. Minimal breakfast if any.
School
8:00am – 1:30pm
The only fully structured window. PE, canteen, timetable — all held by school. This is where HPB's current touchpoints sit.
After School  •  Window 1
2:00 – 6:00pm
~4 hours unsupervised. No Student Care equivalent in secondary. 0 out of 6 parents present. Screen, snacks, peer influence fill the gap.
Evening
6:00 – 9:00pm
Parent presence returns but limited. Dinner if family eats together. Homework. Some tuition 2–3x/week.
Night  •  Window 2
9:00pm – 1:00am+
Phone in bedroom. No enforcement on bedtime. Sleep is entirely self-managed. Screen use affecting sleep when alone in the room.
Design implication

We must design for the gap — solutions must work even when parents can't see. Peers will shape choices in that gap, so we need to make the healthier option easier in friend settings. And replacement works better than restriction — removing something without a better substitute means youth will find a workaround.

The HMW

"How might we motivate youth (aged 13–16) and their key supporters to build their confidence to start and sustain healthy habits across the 6 Wells, so that healthier choices become natural, even as routines change and independence grows?"

Define — Design Principles

Three principles to guide what we build

Each principle resolves a core tension from the research.

01

Autonomy with guardrails

Design for shared structure, not control. Youth decide how things are done and parents work together with them. Together, they create a structure that works for both sides.

02

Replace, don't restrict

Never restrict behaviour without introducing alternatives. Screens and fast food meet emotional and social needs. Remove them without offering alternatives and youth will find workarounds.

03

Make it fit the day they already have

Strengthen existing behaviours before introducing new ones. If it doesn't slot into the after-school hangout, the bedtime window, or the dinner table, it won't get used.

Phase 3 — Co-Creation

From 25+ ideas to 3 concepts

We ran co-creation workshops with 6 youth and 6 parents. Using the opportunity areas as prompts, we presented scenarios and let them ideate what would help. Two key takeaways emerged:

Takeaway 1 — Parents

Parents came in with real self-awareness and a genuine desire to help. But their strategies were built for primary school. "I need to change myself too." They're not the problem. They just need tools that match where their child is now.

Takeaway 2 — Youth

Youth already know what's healthy. What they need is the how. When we asked them to design solutions, every idea changed something around them (the room, the routine) — not something inside them. Not one idea was "try harder."

The shortlisting process

Every idea from co-creation was tested against four filters: North Star (does it move youth toward natural healthy choices?), Design Principles (shared structure, replace not restrict, fit the day?), Design Constraints (no standalone apps, must work through HPB channels, usable at home), and DVF Scoring (desirability, viability, feasibility).

Design — The Three Concepts

Three physical prototypes, each grounded in research

No single concept addresses every well. Each targets specific wells and cascades into others. Together, they form a system of support that answers the HMW across all 6 Wells.

DVF 44/48

Bedtime Wind-Down

Physical magnet board • Youth + Parent • Window 2 (Night)
A physical magnet board where youth design their own wind-down routine. Youth picks the magnets, parent agrees and adds their own commitments. Weekly review built in. Framed as a 5-night experiment, not a rule.

Why this works: The room changes before the youth has to decide anything. A phone in the bedroom is the problem — solving sleep with an app puts the trigger right back in their hands. A physical board stays visible without anyone needing to unlock anything.
Sleep Screen Bond ↻
DVF 38/48

Hook U Up

Physical card deck • Peer pairs • Window 1 (After-school)
A physical deck of challenge cards that two friends do together over 14 days. Topics framed around what youth care about: Glow Up, Bulk Up, Cut Season, Recharge, Energy Era. One card per day, proof uploaded via QR code. No app needed.

Why this works: Youth already challenge each other informally — betting on weight loss, "fining" friends for fast food. This takes existing peer behaviour and gives it structure. Accountability is peer-to-peer, the strongest motivator when no parent is present.
Eat Exercise Screen ↻
DVF 36/48

WOU (We Open Up)

Card game • Youth + Parent • Dinner table
A card game that gets parents and youth talking without it feeling like a lecture. 22 cards across 3 types: 16 category cards (both answer), 2 swap cards (answer as each other), 4 spark cards (go deeper). The simultaneous reveal means neither side dominates.

Why this works: Parents said "everything is about trust." Youth said "you don't trust me?" The "For Now" framing reduces defensiveness because nothing feels permanent. Both sides co-author the agreement.

Design Decision

Why physical, not digital?

All three concepts are deliberately physical objects — magnet boards, card decks, card games. This was a research-driven decision, not an aesthetic one.

Sleep

A phone in the bedroom is the problem

Solving sleep with an app puts the trigger right back in their hands. A physical board stays visible without anyone needing to unlock anything.

After-school

Screen time is already too high

Youth spend their after-school time on screens. A physical card deck displaces screen time rather than adding to it.

Conversation

The medium is the mechanic

The whole point is face-to-face conversation. A digital version removes eye contact, the table, and the shared moment.

Constraint

No standalone apps

HPB's constraint: must work through existing channels. Physical objects are low-cost, printable, and pilot-ready.

The Destination

Our North Star

The North Star

Healthy habits become the natural choice for youth — supported at key moments, shaped through shared routines, and sustained through growing independence.

Empowered Transitions

Support at the moments where habits drift — P6 to Sec 1, Sec 2 to Sec 3, and beyond.

Shared Rhythm

Turn "windows" into shared routines. Build autonomy through small wins that strengthen trust.

Confidence Loop

Youth try something, it works, parents notice, trust grows — and with that, youth gain more freedom.

From Trial to Habit

How short-term testing leads to long-term change

Bedtime Wind-Down

Short term: Youth picks magnets and tries for 5 nights. Parent joins briefly then leaves.

Medium term: Routine sticks on school nights. Weekly reviews feel natural. Youth adjusts magnets as things change.

Long term: Wind-down becomes automatic. The board becomes a connection point, not a control tool.

Hook U Up

Short term: Buddy pair picks a topic deck and does 14 days together. Streak builds momentum.

Medium term: Second topic deck picked. Friends start joining in. Challenge becomes part of the group.

Long term: Healthy behaviours become part of peer identity. Challenge format no longer needed.

WOU (We Open Up)

Short term: First game session. Swap mechanic surprises both sides. They agree on one "For Now" decision.

Medium term: Conversations start happening without the cards. Parent shifts from telling to asking.

Long term: Open communication becomes default. Youth shares pressures willingly. Guidance actually lands.
What we started with vs. what we ended with

We started with 25 ideas and ended with 3. Together, they aim to break the vicious cycle and build the virtuous one — where small wins grow confidence, trust replaces control, and healthy choices start to feel like the natural thing to do. That is the north star.
